What motivates a transfer: player performance, transfer fee or the rivalry between the teams?

Authors

Vignesh Thiruvel Selvam

Prof. Dr.Christoph Ihl

1 Introduction

Teams in the Top 5 Men’s Leagues of Europe (Premier League, La Liga, Bundesliga, Serie A, Ligue 1) are known for their big spending every Summer transfer window. A team that is newly crowned Champion would buy players to continue on their fine form. Teams that got newly promoted would look at options to extend their stay in the top flight. Other teams would try to find players to improve their performance and build upon last season’s results. Thus, no matter where a team finishes, once the Summer transfer window opens, all teams are active in the market. But how do they choose a player? Is it purely based on numbers (stats, age, fee)? Or could there be bigger forces at play, like a player’s nationality or even the rivalry between clubs in a league?

In order to answer this question, we compile a list of teams in the Top 5 leagues in Europe, and their transfer history (only players bought) from the Summer transfer window in 2018 to 2024. We then combine it with a team’s ELO and define a rivalry score. We also make use of a player’s attributes such as their age, nationality, minutes played, etc. along with their transfer fee to determine the motivating factor of a transfer.

2 Data Collection

We obtain league tables and ELO ratings for each season from the following sources:

  • ELO: ClubELO API

  • League tables and Transfers: Scraped from Transfermarkt using WorldFootballR package

The league table for each league gives us the final standings of the teams at the end of each season in our observed range. It gives us the position the team finished in and the points the team has scored that season.

From the ClubELO API, we can get a list of ELO ratings of teams for a given date. We gather the ELO data for the end of each season. We use the team names from both these tables and create a combined table (with points and ELO as required for rivalry measurement) later.

The transfer data consists of players bought by all teams in the top 5 leagues from 2018 to 2024 summer transfer windows. This includes both permanent moves and loan moves (in football, at the end of a loan deal, a player returns to their parent club).

In football, there are 2 transfer windows each season when the teams are allowed to buy players. The first one is the Summer transfer window, usually between June to the end of August before the start of a season. The second window, commonly known as the winter transfer window is usually in the middle of a season in January. For this research we don’t consider transfers in the winter window because:

  1. Winter transfers are based on a team’s position in the middle of a season. Often, it is considered to be a temporary solution (to fill up an injured player’s spot or a desperate transfer to avoid relegation, etc.).
  2. It could even affect a team’s options in the next summer window, due to constrained budget (already spent on players in the winter transfer), overcrowded positions (a player was bought due to an injury to an existing player, but the injured player has now returned, etc.).

Thus, it is a completely different strategy compared to summer transfers and beyond the scope of what we are trying to establish in this analysis.

In addition to the selling club, buying club, their leagues and transfer fee, we also get information about the player such as their age, number of goals scored, minutes played last season, etc. that we use for our analysis.

We then need to filter the transfers to ensure that we only have the data for the arrivals in top 5 leagues (The seller can be from any league. The transfer type need not be permanent). Redundant data also includes the return of a player to his parent club at the end of a loan spell. We remove this as well.

3 Rivalry Measurement

We define competitive rivalry based on the following strategy:

  1. We only measure rivalry between teams that belong to the same league. Teams from different leagues do compete with each other in certain competitions, but are not necessarily considered rivals. Thus, rivalry is meaningful only within a league.

  2. Next, we define a vector:

    \[ \mathbf{x}_{i,t} =\left(z^{(t)}_{i,t},\;z^{(t-1)}_{i,t},\;z^{(t-2)}_{i,t},\;z^{(\mathrm{elo})}_{i,t}\right) \]

    where:

    \(x_{i,t}\) is the feature vector for team \(i\) in season \(t\), consisting of standardized (z-scored) performance measures
    \(z^{(t)}_{i,t}\): is the points in season \(t\)
    \(z^{(t-1)}_{i,t}\): is the points in season \(t-1\)
    \(z^{(t-2)}_{i,t}\): is the points in season \(t-2\)
    \(z^{(\mathrm{elo})}_{i,t}\): is the ELO rating at the start of season \(t\)
    \(d_{ij,t}\): is the rivalry distance between teams \(i\) and \(j\) in season \(t\)

  3. Using this vector, we calculate distances between the teams based on a Modified Euclidean approach.

    \[ d_{ij,t} =\sqrt{\frac{1}{K_{ij,t}}\sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}_{ij,t}}\left(x_{i,k,t} - x_{j,k,t}\right)^2} \]

    where:

    \(d_{ij,t}\) is the distance between teams \(i\) and \(j\) in season \(t\)
    \(\mathcal{K}_{ij,t}\) is the set of valid dimensions used in the comparison: \[ \mathcal{K}_{ij,t} = \left\{ k \;:\; x_{i,k,t} \text{ and } x_{j,k,t} \text{ are both observed} \right\} \] \(K_{ij,t} = \left| \mathcal{K}_{ij,t} \right|\) is the number of valid dimensions

    Note: We do not impute missing data in a vector. We let the distance calculation algorithm take care of this. We have two such cases:

    • We only have the data starting from the end of the 2017/18 season. Thus, for this season, the second and third values in the vector are not present.
    • Also, we could have missing data for a team that was newly promoted to the league.

    In both cases, the above algorithm skips the dimension completely if the dimension is missing for either team.

  4. Next, we attribute this distance score over multiple seasons to smooth out the rivalry score, so that there aren’t drastic changes in rivalry across seasons.

    \[ R^{(s)}_{ij,t}=\alpha \, d_{ij,t}+(1 - \alpha)\, R^{(s)}_{ij,t-1} \] where:

    \(R^{(s)}_{ij,t}\) is the smoothed rivalry score, incorporating past rivalry
    \(\alpha \in (0,1)\) is a smoothing parameter controlling the weight placed on current versus past rivalry. After sensitivity analysis, we set \(\alpha = 0.25\)

  5. Next, we calculate a continuous rivalry proximity score:

    • First, we make the rivalry score symmetric by averaging both directions:

      \[ R^{(\mathrm{sym})}_{ij,t}=\frac{R^{(s)}_{ij,t}+R^{(s)}_{ji,t}}{2} \]

    • Next, we compute a rank-based percentile of the smoothed distance.

    \[ P_{ij,t}=\frac{\operatorname{rank}\left(R^{(\mathrm{sym})}_{ij,t}\right)}{N_t} \] where:

    \(P_{ij,t}\) is the Percentile Rank for a team pair
    \(N_{t}\) is the number of team pairs in that league-season

    • Then, we invert it so that when the score is closer to 1, it means that the two teams are closer rivals.

      \[ \text{Rivalry Proximity}_{ij,t}=1 - P_{ij,t} \tag{1}\]

3.1 Mapping

We have gathered league table, transfers and ELO from different sources. We have merged league table and ELO to create the rivalry vector. But we still need to make sure these team names match with the ones in transfers. Thus, before we can proceed with the analysis, we use fuzzy matching to match team names across tables. The mismatched teams after fuzzy match are stored in a csv file in which we then manually add the correct team name. This file is then used to complete the mapping.

Once the mapping is done, we check again to ensure the data are clean and there are no mismatched sellers. We limit our check for mismatches to sellers from just the top 5 leagues (we don’t have any data for sellers outside the top 5 leagues so these would get falsely flagged as mismatches).

From Table 2 we can clearly see that these are mostly teams mismatched to the league (Ex: Salzburg is a team from the Austrian Bundesliga, but is matched to the German Bundesliga). These erroneous entries can be removed.

Similarly, when we checked for buyers, no mismatches were found.

4 Choice Set Construction

Now, our data are cleaned and ready. Next, we construct choice datasets tailored to the analyses. Each choice set contains:

  • Focal transfer: The observed transfer. The buying team is known as the focal team, and its league is the focal league.

  • Alternative pool: All the other options that the buying team has for the focal transfer. For each focal transfer, the feasible alternatives pool is constructed based on these conditions:

    • The transfer must be in the same window as the focal transfer (this is done using the focal season).

    • The alternative player must play in the same position as the focal player.

    • The transfer fee of the alternative must be comparable to the transfer fee of the focal player. (If the focal player was a free transfer, then a player with an 80 million transfer fee cannot be considered an alternative). Therefore, we restrict the choice set to players whose transfer fees lie within a symmetric bandwidth around the focal player’s fee in logarithmic terms.

      \[ \left| \log(\text{fee}_j) - \log(\text{fee}_i) \right| \leq h \]

      where:

      \(fee_i\): transfer fee of the focal player
      \(fee_j\): transfer fee of the alternative player
      \(h\): the bandwidth parameter governing the admissible range of transfer fees (we set h = 1 after sensitivity analysis)

These choice sets are identified by the choice_id. For each choice set, the transfer with chosen = 1 is the focal transfer. The other transfers in the choice set are marked with chosen = 0 and are the alternatives for the focal transfer. We do not consider transfers with no alternative options.

These are the independent variables that we use in our analysis:

  • player_age :age of the player during the transfer.

  • minutes_played :the number of confirmed minutes the player has played in the previous season.

  • nat_match: denotes if a player (focal/ alternative) belongs to the focal country.

  • goals: the number of goals a player scored the previous season.

  • log_fee: The transfer fee of the player in log scale

  • rivalry_proximity: The rivalry score we calculated based on Equation 1. We compute this score for the focal team against the alternative transfer’s selling team (Sometimes referred to as just rivalry in this article).

The dependent variable is chosen. It is a binary variable, where the value is 1 if the player was chosen, 0 if not.

4.3 Choice Dataset 3

In choice dataset 2 we include alternatives where the seller is from the focal league (but the buyer may be from a different league). For these alternatives, since the seller is from the focal league, the rivalry between the alternative seller and the focal buyer is non-zero. But there are also cases where a team buys players from outside the focal league. Here, the rivalry is always zero between the focal buyer and sellers outside the focal league.

Thus, any effect that rivalry_proximity has may therefore not reflect genuine preference/avoidance. Instead, it could be picking up: “chosen transfers tend to have cross-league sellers (rivalry = 0) while unchosen within-league-seller alternatives have rivalry > 0.” The model would be learning “same-league seller pair vs. cross-league seller pair” rather than “rivalry matters.”

To test this, we now construct a choice dataset by excluding all transfers from outside the focal league (thus all focal and alternative transfers are within the same league). If the result is the same as the model designed using the previous choice set, then the effect can be considered genuine.

5 Model And Results

For our analysis, we need to model why a chosen player was preferred over all other feasible alternatives. Thus, we use the Conditional Multinomial Logit Model.

\[ P_{ij} =\frac{\exp(X_{ij}\beta)}{\sum_{k \in C_i} \exp(X_{ik}\beta)} \]

where:

\(i\): choice situation (choice_id)
\(C_i\): Choice set for \(i\)
\(j\): jth alternative in \(C_i\)
\(P_{ij}\): probability that alternative \(j\) is chosen from choice set \(C_i\)
\(X_{ij}\): observed characteristics (independent variables)

For the choice datasets that we have constructed so far we estimate a model for each set:

5.1 Model 1

Model 1 uses choice dataset 1 (permanent arrivals to the top 5 leagues in the focal group and the buyers of the alternatives are from the same league as the focal buyer)

Code 
model_1 <- clogit(
  chosen ~ player_age +
    minutes_played + 
    nat_match +
    goals +
    log_fee + 
    rivalry_proximity + 
    strata(choice_id),
  data = choice_dataset_1
)

Based on the log likelihood test score and low p-value, this model is statistically significant and provides a reasonable fit. The concordance, although low, is still > 50%, suggesting our model performs better than random selection. So we can look into the coefficients to find out what the model has learned.

Using Table 6 we can investigate the independent variables. For rivalry proximity, we have:

coef ≈ 0.103.
exp(coef) ≈ 1.109.
p ≈ 0.26

This effect is insignificant (p-value > 0.05).

Now we look at other factors that might affect the choice of a team when looking for a player. When we check for player_age:

coef ≈ 0.038.
exp(coef) ≈ 1.039.
p < 0.001

This is statistically significant (low p-value). A one-year increase in a player’s age leads to a 3.9% increase in the odds of selection. Although this isn’t the absolute probability of being signed, it means that clubs value players with more experience (proxied by age) conditioned on fee.

We have another significant variable in minutes_played:

coef ≈ 0.
exp(coef) ≈ 1.
p ≈ 0.019

This is statistically significant (low p-value). But the effect is negligible.

There are other factors such as goals, nationality match and transfer fee, but when we restrict the focal and alternative pool to only include players that arrive to the focal league, these factors are insignificant. This might change when we also include players who left the focal league in the same window.

Note: all effects on all models include the condition that the fee range must be similar for the focal and its alternatives. Thus, the effect is not absolute. It is conditional on price.

5.3 Model 3

For the third model, we use choice dataset 3, which is used to verify the robustness of the outputs of model 2. From model 2, we found out that rivalry has a negative effect on a transfer. As explained earlier in Choice Dataset 3, this could be a result of cross-league transfers having zero rivalry. If model 3 gives a significant negative coefficient for rivalry_proximity, then the effect is genuine. We compare the two models:

Code 
model_3 <- clogit(
  chosen ~ player_age +
    minutes_played + nat_match +
    goals +
    log_fee + 
    rivalry_proximity + 
    strata(choice_id),
  data = choice_dataset_3
)

We can see in Table 8 that although the effect of rivalry is significant here as well, it has the opposite effect on a transfer choice to what model 2 depicted. Here, we get a positive effect of 65.8%. This means that the effect we saw from rivalry_proximity on a player being chosen in model 2 is indeed driven by the fact that cross-seller transfers have zero rivalry effect. Therefore, this effect can be disregarded.

6 Discussion & Conclusion

Overall, we find that a player’s age is an important driving factor, and that teams value a player slightly more if they have more experience for the same price range. Also, clubs consider player availability (minutes played), but it doesn’t seem to have a great deal of effect on the choice. Additionally, when the teams consider players who either leave or join their league as options, they seem to value a player who is familiar with the country more.

Other factors such as goals and rivalry do not appear to matter significantly, as the negative effect from rivalry when the choice set is included both arrivals and departures from the league seems to be merely an artifact arising from the fact that teams from different leagues don’t consider themselves as rivals. Assuming that teams consider only alternatives that fit their budget, cost doesn’t seem to be the decisive factor as long as the players fit the other criteria.
